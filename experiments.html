Introduction

Synthesizing DNA origami in a lab environment is a multi-step process. It is necessary to do so to observe how our origami is forming, which will determine its efficacy and possible applications in the future. Below we outline our steps and the protocols for constructing our csDNA.

Figure 1:A schematic showing the steps of creating DNA origami in the lab. Upon aliquoting the individual DNA strands, we anneal our origami in a PCR machine to ensure that it forms together. It then undergoes purification to filter out any remaining single-stranded DNA. The origami can then be used for imaging preparation in a process called flow. Image made in BioRender.

Our csDNA was annealed in a thermal cycler (SimpliAmp), heated up to 90 °C for 5 minutes followed by gradual cooling to 4 °C at a rate of 0.1 °C every 6 seconds. Our robot and inhibitor complex were annealed separately. The robot and inhibitor were added at a 1:1 ratio and diluted with 10X TAE and MQ water. This complex was heated to 90 °C in 5 minutes and then cooled to 4 °C at a rate of 0.1 °C every 19.2 seconds. The annealed robot-inhibitor complex was then added to the csDNA, and the two were annealed together at a 1:1 ratio.

Purification Protocol

Following annealing, our csDNA and robot were purified using PEG purification. 100 μl of the origami was spun with 100 μl of PEG 8000, pH 8 at 10,000 RPM for 30 minutes. 180 μl of supernatant was removed, and 90 μl of 1X TAE and 90 μl of PEG 8000 were added and spun again. This was repeated 3 times to purify our solution.

NanoDrop

The concentration of our csDNA and robot-inhibitor complex was measured using a spectrophotometer (Nanodrop 2000, ThermoFisher Scientific). 2 μl of our sample was loaded, and the concentration was recorded 3 times to achieve a reliable reading. After determining the concentration of the csDNA, it was diluted to 0.2 nM n Buffer B+.

Imaging Protocol

In order to image our DNA origami samples, we used Total Internal Reflection Fluorescence (TIRF) microscopy, with a ONI Nanoimager, Oxford Nanoimaging. To capture single-color DNA-PAINT for our samples, we used the green channel, the wavelength of which corresponded to that of our Cy3B dye. The imaging settings we used are as follows:

Data Analysis

The output from our microscope imaging was a TIFF file, which underwent several steps of data analysis to achieve the final image. The steps are as follows:

  1. Input the TIFF file into ImageJ. Crop the image at the coordinates X = 90, Y = 180 with a size of 256 x 256 pixels.
  2. Input the resulting cropped file into Picasso Localize. This will localize and identify each individual spot in the image, allowing us to know where our origami is located. Use the following parameters for fitting the localization spots:
Parameter Value
Camera Sensitivity 0.46
Quantum Efficiency 0.82
Pixel Size 117

Note: The gradient should be adjusted to minimize overlap between the localization boxes.

  1. Input the HDF5 file into Picasso Render to carry out drift correction. Select Undrift from RCC, and adjust the segmentation parameter. This will employ an algorithm to undrift the entirety of the image. Further minor drift correction can be carried out by selecting individual markers in the image and undrifting from the picked spots.

These are the steps to produce the final DNA-PAINT image of our DNA origami.

Dye Conjugation

We purchased our imager strands and dyes separately from IDT. In order to have imager strands ready for use in DNA-PAINT, it was necessary to conjugate them together. The steps for dye conjugation are outlined below:

  1. Add dimethyl sulphoxide (DMSO), an aprotic solvent, to Cy3b fluorophore NHS ester. Add 1x to a 10x solution to create a standard solution of 1 mg/ml solution.
  2. Dilute the imager solution to 1mM by adding LCMS water. The imager strand-to-fluorophore ratio should be 1:10.
  3. The product should be diluted to 40 μl using LCMS water. The imager concentration should be 0.5 μM. Add sodium bicarbonate to the imager strand solution at 0.1x concentration.
  4. Add the fluorophore and DMSO mix in a 500 μl tube. Vortex the solution and mix continuously. Add water drop by drop to avoid an exothermic reaction that will result in evaporation of the fluorophores.
  5. Cover the solution using parafilm and keep it in the Eppendorf Thermomixer for 18 hours at 30 ℃ at 900 RPM.

After conjugating the dye, we had to perform HPLC purification to isolate the different oligonucleotides based on their physical properties. The steps of HPLC purification are explained below:

  1. The HPLC machine (1260 Infinity II, Agilent) consists of 5 modules - A) Quaternary Palm B) Sample Injector C) Column Oven D) Multiple Wavelength Detector E) Fraction Collector. Upon completing the conjugation reaction, the sample is stored in the injection pump at A1.
  2. The hydrophobic column is connected to the quaternary pump, which is connected to the solvents TEAA (Tris Ammonium Acetate - hydrophilic) and Acetonitrile (hydrophobic). 1.5 ml tubes are added to the fraction collector to collect samples.
  3. The quaternary pump flows TEAA for 25 minutes with 95% solvent flow. The column is then cleaned with 100% acetonitrile, and equilibrium is maintained at 5% TEAA and 95% acetonitrile.
  4. The Multiple Wavelength Detector has wavelengths set to 260 and 280 nm for DNA amine and 500 nm for Cy3b (which can vary depending on the absorbance peaks of the fluorophore).
  5. Fraction Collector method and signals are set along with threshold to 100 mAU (milli absorbance unit) to filter out buffers and other contents, to concentrate fluorophores and DNA amines.
  6. The analytic run is 15 μl or 3 nanomoles. Upon completing the process, the rest of the sample is injected and the method is run again.

Once HPLC purification is completed, the following steps are carried out to collect a final stock of imager strands.

  1. The imager strands are freeze dried by placing the sample in dry ice and then in a vacuum pump overnight.
  2. Nanodrop analysis is carried out to determine the concentration of the imager. MQ water is added to dilute the sample to the required concentration.
